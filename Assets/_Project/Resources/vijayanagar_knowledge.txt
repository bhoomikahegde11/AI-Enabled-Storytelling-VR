In the bustling market districts of the 16th century Vijayanagar Empire merchants and buyers move among stalls beneath woven awnings, trading in a layered currency system where the Varaha is the dominant large silver coin, Fanams are the smaller gold or silver units used for everyday purchases, and Kasu are the copper coins for small change — merchants commonly work with conversions (approximately 1 Varaha ≈ 16 Fanams, 1 Fanam ≈ 4 Kasu) to make exact change during rapid trades. Spice stalls sell goods by traditional weight units and by reputation: black pepper and cardamom command high value for export and elite households, turmeric and ginger are everyday staples, while sandalwood and aromatic powders are treated as luxury items for temples and nobles. Buyers arrive from many strata — imperious court nobles, seasoned foreign merchants, temple priests, village farmers, and lowly street sellers — and each brings different expectations and knowledge of fair pricing. Bargaining is a practiced dance: opening offers are often strategic, counters are made with ritualized phrases and gestures, and public reputation, subtle flattery, and the tone one uses can move a deal as much as the coins on the table. The market's soundscape is a chorus of haggling, the clink of coins, and stalls calling out wares; merchants use quick mental arithmetic and social cues to close deals, while respect, appearance, and perceived fairness shape long-term relationships between sellers and repeat customers.
